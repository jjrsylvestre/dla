:: Scheduling and Using Discover Linear Algebra

The Augustana Campus of the University of Alberta has a somewhat unusual calendar: each of Fall and Winter are split into a 3-week "block" term consisting of 13 instructional days with 3 hours of instruction per day, followed by a more "traditional" 11-week term. I have taught Linear Algebra I using Discover Linear Algebra in both types of term, but have only taught Linear Algebra II using Discover Linear Algebra in 11-week terms. Below I give my typical schedules for both courses.

Note that these schedules are built on the assumption of a flipped classroom, where the majority of class time is devoted to the Discovery Guide activities and students watch lecture videos afterwards. In years before I had prepared lecture videos for this purpose, instead I scheduled every third or fourth class to be a "consolidation lecture" where I would go back over the concepts, examples, and theory from the previous two or three chapters. There are many different ways you could make this sort of thing work, depending on the duration of each class meeting and how many total hours of contact time you have over your term. Experiment and see what works for you and your students!

Note that it is not necessary (nor is it really intended) for every student to complete every problem of every Discovery Guide. The point is the journey, not the destination. As long as the students are engaged, focussed, and making reasonable progress during a class period devoted to discovery activities, the "gaps" can be filled in with textbook reading and/or consolidation/video lectures.

A Typical Discovery Day

  • I usually begin with a short introduction to get their minds back into linear algebra mode: a short recap of last time, examples, some bit of theory that deserves highlighting, motivation for the day's new topic, some important fact(s) they'll need to recall to make good progress on the day's new activities. (Don't try to cram all of this into every start-of-class intro! Pick and choose what is appropriate for that day, taking care not to take too much time centred on the instructor and away from what's really important: student engagement in their own learning.)
  • Then I let them loose on the new Discovery Guide in groups, encouraging them to be loud and ask lots of questions (of me, of their peers). Throughout this time I walk around the room answering questions, giving encouragement, prodding students who are not engaging, etc. I let groups work ahead at their own pace, but to ensure that the whole class eventually progresses together I give periodic time limits: "In five minutes we'll come back together and discuss Activities X and Y."
  • Periodically during the class meeting I will interrupt the group work to have a brief whole-class discussion of both the "answers" and the "point" (or learning objective) of a handful of activities that most students should have completed by that point. Then I let them loose again to make more progress from that point.
  • In the final 3 to 5 minutes of the period I wrap up with some "big picture" learning objectives for the topic.
